In The Brothers Karamazov, Dostoevsky compares two types of people: *Worldly People and Monks.* Worldly People chase pleasure and comfort, but in doing so, they lose their freedom. On the other hand, Monks renounce pleasure and, as a result, remain  truly free. But why is that the case? According to Dostoevsky, freedom is not just the ability to do whatever you want, but the ability to endure suffering. If you can’t endure suffering, you become a slave to anything that protects you from it. *Worldly People* believe freedom lies in pleasure, so they dedicate their lives to acquiring wealth, status, luxury, and comfort. However, when these things are taken away, they suffer—and since they don’t know how to handle suffering, they become desperate to escape it, serving anyone who promises to restore their comfort.

Monks, on the other hand, train themselves to withstand discomfort and detach from worldly pleasures. They remain unaffected when they lose material things like money or status because their freedom doesn’t rely on external comfort. The key to enduring suffering lies in finding meaning in it. Viktor Frankl, in *Man’s Search for Meaning*, wrote that suffering ceases to be suffering the moment it finds meaning. But here’s the question: Does all suffering have meaning? The answer isn’t clear, and perhaps it never will be.

In the end, believing that suffering has meaning is a personal choice. If you choose to believe it does, you’ll be able to endure hardship and stay free. But if you believe suffering is meaningless, the logical response is to escape it through pleasure. The problem? When tragedy strikes and you lose the comfort you built for yourself, you’ll end up surrendering your freedom to the first person who can offer you relief.

This raises an important question: If the world needed someone to stand up against tyranny, who would be more reliable? Worldly People, who can be easily bribed with comfort, or Monks, who remain unaffected by the temptations of power and wealth? The choice is yours!
