Mass psychosis is an epidemic of madness that occurs when a large portion of society loses touch with reality and descends into delusions.

What causes a mass psychosis?

What drives an individual mad?
Drug/alcohol, brain injury, and other illness. A flood of negative emotions leads to madness.

Mass psychosis is primarily caused by psychogenic triggers, particularly a flood of negative emotions like fear and anxiety. When individuals are instead of panic they seek relief, which can be achieved through adaptive means or by undergoing a psychotic break. A psychotic break is a reordering of one's experiential world that blends fact and fiction helping to alleviate panic. Isolation and disruption of normal social interactions also contribute to the susceptibility to delusions.

## Psychogenic
Psychogenic diseases are a group of symptoms or complaints that likely originate from complex interactions within the brain's frontal lobes and the environment in which these complaints occur. These disorders often arise from mental or emotional conflicts.

## Totalitarianism
Totalitarianism is a form of government that attempts to assert total control over the lives of its citizens. It is characterized by a strict central rule that attempts to control and direct all aspects of individual life through coercion and repression. It does not permit individual freedom. Totalitarian states typically have the following characteristics:
 - **A single ruling party or leader:** Totalitarian status is not democracy, and they do not allow for any real political opposition.
 - **State control of the economy:** Totalitarian states often have a centrally planned economy, in which the government controls all aspects of production and distribution.
 - **State control of media:** Totalitarian states control the media to control
information flow to the public. This allows them to suppress dissent and propaganda.
- **Repression of dissent:** Totalitarian states use a variety of methods to repress dissent, including violence, intimidation, and imprisonment.
