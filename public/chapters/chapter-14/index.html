Chapter 14 — Marcus Aurelius

Marcus Aurelius - the author of meditations

Marcus Aurelius Antoninus, known simply as Marcus Aurelius, portrays himself in his writings as a mature male, a dedicated statesman, and the ruler of Rome. At the time he penned this straightforward self-description, he occupied the esteemed position of emperor. Born on April 26, AD 121, in Rome as Marcus Annius Verus, he would go on to shape the course of history until his death on March 17, 180 AD, at the northern frontiers of the Roman Empire.

Throughout his reign, which lasted nearly two decades, Marcus Aurelius managed to balance the immense responsibilities of leadership with a life that was largely private and introspective. Despite facing relentless external pressures and challenges from all sides ranging from military conflicts to political strife he was still able to set aside time for reflection and self-improvement. This inner dialogue culminated in the writings collectively known as “Meditations.”

Unlike personal diaries or private journals, these reflections stand out as profound contemplations, often characterized by a conversational tone with a divided self. These reflections serve a dual purpose: as a reminder to his own soul and as a reflection on the Stoic philosophy that guided his life. Throughout the text, it is clear that Marcus engages in deep introspection, examining his thoughts and feelings, often finding himself responding to the turmoil of his mind rather than offering straightforward answers. The result is a remarkable testament to his character, portraying a ruler grappling with his own humanity while striving to uphold the ideals of Stoicism amidst the complexities of his reign.
